We can look at airline routes between some of the major airports. With the recent Russian invasion of the Ukraine, countries from around the world have closed their airspaces to Russian aircraft. Russia has responded similarly to such moves. The purpose of our map is to see how airspace closures of a given country can alter or impact the major air routes normally used between major airports. Would airlines need to make use of a midway point in order to go around a particular country? In other situations natural disasters and the transportation of dignitaries can cause the halt of civilian flights to a particular airport^1. If an airport is not available for landing, what would be the closest major airport that civilian aircraft can use (especially if it is a long flight which needs to refuel). Our preliminary thought to answering this kind of question would be: what would the world’s airspace look like if we closed off airspaces above individual countries? We know generally that airplanes fly in great circle routes from their departing to destination airports. What does large rerouting of flights look like and what are the impacts or costs of having an air route “For every aircraft [an] optimal flight plan exists (it can be fuel-optimal or time-optimal or optimal according to another objective) which includes both the altitude profile and the speed at every point along the path” (Kostitsyna et al. 2014). Ideally the direct and shortest path from an origin to a destination, is something which air operators strive for as it offers the lowest possible fuel consumption, and usually the shortest travel time. However, “choosing the most optimal route, that would allow the operator to minimize fuel consumption, is one of the most important challenges in the contemporary air transportation branch” (Majka et al. 2014). Making such matters even more complex is the fact that flight routes are susceptible to political conflicts which may result in airspace closures. Flight Information Regions (or FIRs), are established for air traffic control purposes (Flight information regions, 2012), and the responsibility to oversee these are given to the closest countries. Smaller countries tend to have one FIR, while larger countries have many. According to UNCLOS (United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea) airspace sovereignty extends only 12 nautical miles from the coastline of a particular country. The FIR though extends much further. As we see in the map BELOW?, which shows the flight route of an Aeroflot Flight Route between Moscow and Kaliningrad, before and after the airspace ban imposed by the Baltic and Scandinavian countries, the actual ban only applied to an area within the 12 miles from the coastline shown on the map, but the flight path taken by Aeroflot flew on the boundary of the FIR administered by different countries, much further away from the actual sovereign airspace of the individual countries. It was only for a small portion of the flight route, in which the Aeroflot flights actually flew within the FIRs of two countries which had imposed an airspace ban. These two countries were Lithuania and Latvia, and while Aeroflot flights continued to fly within the FIR of these countries the flight route remained outside of the actual sovereign airspace of these countries. This presents us with an interesting question. Why were Russian airlines such as Aeroflot mostly avoiding flight within the FIRs of the countries and primarily only flying along the boundaries of the FIRs, even though the airspace ban was only legally valid upto 12 miles from the coastline? While we may not have the answers for such a question, we can look more into other cases where this has occurred. Airspace closures for political reasons are not uncommon, and leading up to the recent airspace bans over the Russian invasion of Ukraine, such acts seem to have increased in recent years. According to Majka et al, “in over its 100-year history, aviation has witnessed many phenomena of airspace bypass due to safety or political issues. One of the most iconic and interesting cases [was] the Soviet airspace during the Cold War, which was very restricted to other countries, resulting in airlines not being allowed to overfly it. As a result, in order to maintain connection between Asian and European countries, flights had to follow polar routes and with the aircraft and their technology at the time, were forced to perform a stopover and refuel in Anchorage in Alaska” (2014). In 2017 an airspace closure was imposed against Qatar-registered aircraft by Egypt, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia and United Arab Emirates. This resulted in Qatari aircraft to “perform deviations on the routes to Europe and Africa in order to bypass the airspaces of afore-mentioned states” (Majka et al, 2014). These deviations were no doubt costly for Qatar-based airlines which had to use Iranian airspace towards the east, as all airspace towards the west of the country was restricted. This meant that all flights westward from Qatar, had to take longer paths around the Arab bloc countries. In 2020, Qatar Airways even sought a $5 billion compensation from Arab bloc over the airspace ban imposed against it (Cornwell 2020). Then in 2021, the forced landing of a Ryanair flight 4978 over Belarus airspace resulted in a Safety Directive from the European Union Safety Agency (EASA) instructing flight operators to not fly in the Belarussian airspace due to safety reasons. Finally, in 2022 we see various airspace bans imposed over the Russian invasion of Ukraine. Clearly there has been an increase in airspace restrictions due to political issues. How this has had an impact on flight operations and would impact flight operations in the future is still something to be determined. How airline companies have responded to airspace closures is definitely something which we can explore more in depth and study carefully through data visualization on maps. The distance traveled and travel time from an origin to a destination are important pieces of information which may be useful to airline companies, especially when it comes to determining shortest alternative routes during times when fuel prices are high.
